I frequently meet budding entrepreneurs, people with ideas, looking for a technical co-founder, or someone to build them the thing they have in mind.

Sometimes, 6 months or a year later, they’d drop me a note, “Hey remember X? I’ve finally found someone to build it! It’s still early, but can you look at it? I’d appreciate your feedback” I’d click on the link or look at their source code…

And my heart sinks.

How much have you spent on this (first time developer?) freelancer… and ohh, the time. Wasted. One year wasted only to reach here.

This happens heart-breakingly often.

Though I may not have been sold on an idea, I still wish, a year ago, I had set them off with a decent running app that does the gist of things. Good foundation, fully tested, deployed on the internet, in proper markup for future designers to extend, ready for their first users and iteration - which I’m sure they’d get to, these guys have the heart and the energy.

If only they’d have at least that to hustle with. A year ago.

Well, let’s change that

First, we must realize that Parkinson’s Law is very, very, very true. For everyone.

“Work expands so as to fill the time available for its completion” - Parkinson’s Law

If you’re still in the early stages, the gist of your idea can usually be done quickly. The more time you lend to it, the more you procrastinate, the more unfocused, airy fairy it gets, the less likely it ever gets built.

Bringing your idea into existence quickly helps you move beyond theory. Let real people experience it, and give real feedback. Then you iterate and improve on it quickly.

That’s the reason shipping fast and frequently is so critical. Until your product or feature is out in the wild, being used by real users, you’re building in a tunnel of assumptions. You need empirical proof what you’re working on will pay off. And the only proof you’ll get is with usage. - Josh Pigford, How Baremetrics went from $0 to $14,000 per month
